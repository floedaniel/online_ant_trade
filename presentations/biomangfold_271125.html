Q13: Is Anoplolepis gracilipes versatile in its habitat use?

Answer: Anoplolepis gracilipes, commonly known as the yellow crazy ant, is indeed versatile in its habitat use. The species has demonstrated adaptability to a wide range of environments, including rainforests, savanna woodlands, dry vine thickets, monsoon rainforests, and urban areas (Hoffmann & Saul, 2010; Rajesh et al., 2020). It thrives in both recently invaded areas with small seedlings and older sites with larger vegetation, indicating its ability to utilize diverse microhabitats for nesting and foraging (Abbott & Abbott, 2005). The ant’s presence in various ecological conditions, such as the absence of red land crabs and the presence of thick leaf litter, further illustrates its adaptability (Abbott & Abbott, 2005). Additionally, A. gracilipes can sustain high densities in supercolonies, such as those on Christmas Island, and exhibits continuous foraging activity across different environments, including understorey and canopy trees (Abbott & Abbott, 2005). Its ability to thrive in both urban and less-urbanized landscapes, despite the competitive nature of local ant communities, underscores its versatility (Rajesh et al., 2022).

Q15: Does Anoplolepis gracilipes compete with other species for resources?

Answer: Yes, Anoplolepis gracilipes competes with other species for resources. It engages in both interference and exploitation competition for floral nectar against other invasive ant species such as Linepithema humile and Pheidole megacephala, visiting inflorescences more frequently and consuming more nectar (Lach et al., 2005). Additionally, A. gracilipes competes with native terrestrial hermit crabs for carrion on tropical islands, driving them away from resources by attacking them and spraying formic acid, which alters the trophic ecology of the ecosystem (McNatty et al., 2009). It also competes with native ant species and impacts the abundance and diversity of endemic arboreal species in the Seychelles, suggesting competitive interactions for food and habitat resources (Kaiser-Bunbury et al., 2014).
